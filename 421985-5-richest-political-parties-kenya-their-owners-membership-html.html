Kenya has been a multi-party state since 1992 when the then-president Daniel arap Moi, in December 1991 at a KANU delegates meeting at Kasarani Stadium, repealed Section 2A of the constitution, thereby ending the one-party rule.

The change enabled the introduction of term limits to the presidency.

According to the Office of the Registrar of Political Parties, there were 73 fully registered political parties in Kenya as of May 2021.

Since 2007, two coalitions, Jubilee and the now defunct National Super Alliance (NASA), have dominated the general elections with the largest membership in parliament.

Parties form coalitions to enhance chances of achieving certain goals, such as winning elections and forming a government or strengthening their role as opposition.

Sources of political parties funding

Section 23 of the Political Parties Act establishes the Political Parties Fund that forms the basis for political parties funding in Kenya.

The Political Parties Fund was established to entrench democracy in Kenya.

The sources of the Political Parties Fund are:

  • Funds not less than 0.3% of the revenue collected by the national government as Parliament may provide.
  • Membership fees.
  • Voluntary contributions from a lawful source
  • Donations, bequests and grants from a lawful source.

However, political parties in Kenya currently receive 0.03% of the revenue collected despite the provision of 0.3% in the law.

Jubilee secretary-general Raphael Tuju said the amount was too low.

"The allocation is below what is provided for in the law. There are grey areas in the law and if we were to start agitating for more money as the ruling party, we would appear insensitive to the taxpayers,” Tuju was quoted by The Standard.

Distribution of political funds

Not all political parties in Kenya are entitled to the allocation of funds from the exchequer. Parties that do not secure at least 3% of the total number of votes in the general election usually miss out.

Richest, largest political parties

1. Jubilee

Jubilee is the ruling political party in Kenya.

It was founded on September 8, 2016, following the merger of 11 smaller parties.

The party leader Uhuru Kenyatta was re-elected president in the 2017 General Election, and his running mate, William Ruto, deputy president.

During the 2017 election, the Jubilee Party secured 140 seats in the National Assembly and 24 in the Senate.

The party gets its funding from elected members who pay monthly subscription fees.

In the period preceding the August 2017 General Election, Jubilee, in its audited accounts, reported it had slightly under KSh 1.4 billion in its account.

The party said KSh 1.11 billion was collected from members while KSh 621.8 million was raised from nomination fees.

In July 2021, Jubilee received KSh 353.8 million from the National Treasury as a share of its political parties fund.

However, the party's fortunes began dwindling in 2019, with different factions loyal to the president and his deputy fighting.

Ruto's allies have since openly embraced the United Democratic Alliance (UDA).

Jubilee Party claims it has 8.25 million registered members.

2. Orange Democratic Movement (ODM)

The ODM party was formed during the 2005 Kenyan constitutional referendum campaign pitting the orange and banana sides.

The outfit is one of the stable political parties in the country that has taken part in all elections since 2007.

During the 2017 General Election, ODM bagged 62 seats in the National Assembly and 13 in the Senate. ODM claims it has 4.5 million members.

The party has received KSh 487 million from Treasury since 2017.

Out of the total amount, it retained KSh 334.5 million. At the same time, it agreed to share KSh 153 million with its former partners, Wiper, Amani National Congress (ANC), Ford Kenya and Chama cha Mashinani (CCM).

In the 2016/2017 financial year, the party led by Raila Odinga revealed it collected KSh 434 million from nominations and contributions from members during the busy campaign period.

In July 2021, the second-largest party in Kenya received 165.2 million from Treasury.

The ODM party's assets were worth KSh 25.3 million in 2010.

3. Wiper

The Wiper Democratic Movement-Kenya was founded in 2006 and is led by former vice president Kalonzo Musyoka.

In the 2017 General Election, Wiper got 19 members in the National Assembly and two in the Senate.

The opposition party was part of the defunct National Super Alliance and often fought with ODM over funding.

Wiper received KSh 70.4 million from ODM in July 2021.

4. Amani Nation Congress

Former vice president Musalia Mudavadi founded the Amani Nation Congress (ANC) party in 2015.

It is dominant in Western Kenya and secured 12 seats in the National Assembly and two seats in the Senate in 2017.

The outfit took home KSh 43.8 million from ODM in July 2021, part of the funds disbursed by the exchequer since 2017.

5. Ford Kenya

The Forum for the Restoration of Democracy (FORD) was founded in August 1991 by Jaramogi Oginga Odinga, Phillip Gachoka, Ahmed Bamahariz, Salim Ndamwe, Masinde Muliro and George Nthenge.

It was, however, outlawed by the KANU strongman Daniel Moi before the entry of multi-party politics.

In 1992, it split into two factions due to internal wrangles. One faction (Ford-Asili) was led by the late Kenneth Matiba while the other (Ford Kenya) by the late Jaramogi Oginga.

Currently, the party is led by Bungoma senator Moses Wetang'ula who is fighting for its control with Kanduyi MP Wafula Wamunyinyi.

The outfit won 10 MP seats in 2017.

Ford-Kenya received KSh 36.04 million from ODM in July 2017 before it announced its exit from the NASA coalition.

6. United Democratic Alliance

The revamped UDA party claims to have over 100 MPs allied to it after some of its members decamped from the ruling Jubilee Party.

The party claims it has registered over two million members since December 2020 when it was formed.

Deputy President William Ruto had indicated he would use UDA as his political vehicle in the race to State House in the 2022 polls.
