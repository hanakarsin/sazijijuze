Tanzanians allow Ugandan delegation to port but block Kenyans

March 2024 · 3 minute read

- The Kenyan delegation were together with the Ugandan group touring the port of Tanga which has been proposed as a potential exit point for Uganda’s crude oil exports

- Energy Cabinet Secretary Charles Keter and the Kenyan officials had their passports siezed by the Tanzanian authorities and were blocked from entering the port

- Tanzanians only allowed the Uganda delegation into the port

On Wednesday, March 23 Tanzanian officials sized Kenya's Energy Cabinet Secretary Charles Keter's passport and denied him and other Kenyan government officials access to the port of Tanga.

The top Kenyan officials had began their journey in Lamu, where they inspected the proposed Lamu port before flying to the port of Tanga in Tanzania.

The Kenyan government officials were in the mission to solve the dispute between Kenya and Uganda over whether a proposed oil pipeline to export Uganda’s oil would pass through Kenya or Tanzania.

The Kenyan officials felt that the Tanzanian authorities were harassing them as they had left  Ugandan  delegation led by Energy minister Irene Muloni to proceed with their tour undisturbed.

Apart from Keter the other Kenyan government officials who had their travel documents seized were Petroleum Principal Secretary Andrew Kamau, his Energy counterpart, Mr Joseph Njoroge and Lapsset CEO Sylvester Kasuku.

Kamau confirmed that their passports were confiscated as they toured the Tanga port which has been proposed as the exit point for Ugandan oil.

Kamau said they only allowed the Ugandan delegation to pass.

Apparently the Tanzanian government was angry for Kenya not inviting  them to Lamu, or to the talks between presidents Uhuru Kenyatta and Uganda’s Yoweri Museveni on Monday, March 21 at state house Nairobi.

Technical teams from Kampala and Nairobi had held discussions in  Kenya  over the Uganda oil pipeline project but failed to come to an agreement on where the pipeline is to pass through.

Talks that were led by Ugandan President Yoweri Museveni and his Kenyan counterpart Uhuru Kenyatta at State House in Nairobi ended without an agreement.

The northern pipeline, proposed by Kenya runs from  Hoima in Uganda through Lokichar, to Lamu.

Museveni raised questions about the possible delay in the construction of the pipeline, the Lamu port and the security situation in Kenya which was seen as the stumbling block to an agreement.

The talks will continue in Kampala within a period of two weeks.

Uganda had earlier agreed with Tanzania that the pipeline will pass through the country locking out Kenya from the lucrative deal.

Uganda expects its first oil refinery to start operations by 2018, the facility will have an initial capacity of 30,000 barrels per day, the aim being to reach 60,000 barrels by 2020.
